SO... WHAT IS THIS ALL ABOUT?

Well, for starters this webpage is for the CS299 class of course. But in general, this project highlights my tech usage over a certain time period. The last week of September, to be specific. Over the course of the week, I tracked various things: the devices I used the most, the apps or websites I frequented, and my feelings regarding my tech usage. I think this project is something that anybody can and should do (if possible of course). During my class so far, we have learned about technomoral virtues and data feminism. If you want to learn more about technomoral virtues, you should check out Shannon Vallor’s book titled the same as well as my first project from this semester.

However, this project is inspired by Catherine D'Ignazio’s and Lauren Klein’s book “*Data Feminism.*” If you couldn’t tell by the title, this book talks about how data has been collected in the past and present and how it is absolutely necessary to contextualize data from an intersectional feminist standpoint. The authors considered there to be 7 core principles of Data Feminism:

1. **Examine power** 2. **Challenge power** 3. **Elevate emotion and embodiment** 4. **Rethink binaries and hierarchies** 5. **Embrace pluralism** 6. **Consider context** 7. **Make labor visible**

These core principles inform the path I took in regards to collecting and contextualizing my own data. My personal motivations for the project were informed by the themes in the Watch Dogs 2 (WD2) video game. “What were the themes in the game,” you may ask. Well, it is important to note that in our technology filled world, our personal data is everywhere all at once (peep the movie reference). Nowadays everyone who has even a slight online presence is tracked through our cookies and search history.

Have you ever received an advertisement for a new DSLR camera after google searching for one just a few hours before. Yup! That’s corporations selling your data to advertisers so you can buy more and more and more. In a way, this process I took over a week was a way to take control of the narrative over my own personal data, similar to how Marcus and Dedsec from WD2 used his knowledge to inform others around them about how their data was being used. I knew going into this that I wouldn’t stop my personal data from being sold, but I can contextualize my usage and habits to better understand myself, my wants, my needs, and daily routine.

Here’s the fun part: I’m going to take you through a journey of my tech usage from my week of exploration. Below, you have the option to choose a device and a part of the week to see how my data was being used. You get to see my journal entries (ooooooooo scary inner thoughts time! Kidding… it’s not thatttt scary lol) as well as my manual data collection. Then you will see my data usage from Spotify and TikTok from this week. Why these two specifically? Well, they are my most used apps! At least, according to my browser history and screentime on my phone. ANYWAY, I’ll stop yapping and start letting you explore MY TECH USAGE.

Have Fun!!

THANKS FOR YOUR VIEWERSHIP

- Thanks for taking this journey with me! There are a few things I learned about myself (hint: what I list below can be seen briefly in my journal entries).

- I am bored… A LOT. And that boredom leads to: Scrolling TikTok - I use Notion A LOT. More than Google Docs and Microsoft Word - I open productivity apps more often but I spend the longest cumulative on my more fun apps like TikTok and AO3 in one stretch. - I use Spotify so much that the only accurate to track my usage is downloading my data - I often feel two emotions at once: neutral and bored, negative and stressed or bored, positive and productive or amused simultaneously

- As for the Data Collection and Research Process I’ve learned:

- there are many ways to visualize data outside of charts and bar graphs. I mean.. obviously - A scary amount of my data is just… there. In the cloud servers of the technology I use. Kinda weird… - Code really really helps make the large amounts of data be condensed and visualized easily - Working with JSONs are a bit easier than you think. Especially once you recognize its similarities to CSV files (aka the things used in spreadsheets) - Speaking of, I learned that you can convert said JSON files to CSV to import into excel. I will definitely use this in the future - Its better to track yourself periodically than all the time every time. I learned this about half way through my research process and wish I applied it sooner. - I did a LOT of research into the coding behind the files I worked with and how it is visualized. It is easier to use python but you can use your pdfs and excel files to create a markdown for your HTML page. I will most definitely use this when I work with data again.

- Overall, this was a very intensive process but I learned a lot about how data is stored and then visualized. Thanks again for taking this journey! Feel free to mess around the webpage (PS I might continue updating this page often just to see how my data has changed or remained the same)

My Questionnaire



So, just to clarify, as part of my evaluation for my manual data, I tracked to the best of my ability my usage of my phone. I asked myself the following questions:
1. When did I open my phone? 2. How long did I use an app for? 3. What were my feelings during this? and 4. Was I bored?
I asked myself these questions because before I started this project, I knew that I often used my devices pretty absentmindedly.
I exported a portion of my journal to an excel sheet then produced charts from the data. I have attached the images of my manual data below! My journal entries and digital traces will come after you see my questionnaire first, as expected of course!
